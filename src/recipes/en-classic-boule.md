A timeless sourdough boule with an open crumb and blistered crust. This recipe follows a cold-retard method for maximum flavour development — most of the active work is in the first afternoon, then the dough does its thing overnight in the fridge.

## Steps

### Feed your starter

duration: 240

Feed your starter with equal parts flour and water (e.g. 50g starter + 50g flour + 50g water). Leave at room temperature until doubled and showing active bubbles — usually 3–4 hours at 22°C.

**Float test:** drop a small spoonful in water. If it floats, you're ready to bake.

### Autolyse

duration: 60

Combine the bread flour, whole wheat flour, and **90% of your water** (reserve the rest for later). Mix until no dry flour remains — about 1 minute. Cover and rest for 45–60 minutes.

This rest allows gluten to develop without kneading and makes the dough much easier to work with.

### Mix in the starter

duration: 15

Add the ripe starter to the autolysed dough. Squeeze it through your fingers until fully incorporated. The dough will feel loose at first — keep going for 3–5 minutes until smooth and cohesive.

### Add salt

duration: 5

Dissolve the salt in the reserved water, then pour over the dough. Fold and squeeze until fully incorporated. The dough should feel smooth and slightly elastic.

### Bulk fermentation with folds

duration: 210-300

Transfer to a lightly oiled container. Over the next 2–3 hours, perform **4–5 sets of stretch-and-folds** spaced 30 minutes apart:

1. Wet your hand, grab one side of the dough, stretch it up and fold it over to the opposite side.
2. Rotate the container 90° and repeat. Do this 4 times around the dough — that's one set.

After the last fold, leave the dough undisturbed. It's ready when it has increased by 50–75% in volume, has a domed top, and wobbles when you shake the container.

### Pre-shape

duration: 10

Turn the dough onto an unfloured surface. Using a bench scraper and one hand, drag the dough towards you with quick, confident strokes to build surface tension into a rough ball. Leave uncovered for 20–30 minutes — the surface will dry slightly, which helps the final shape hold.

### Final shape

duration: 10

Lightly flour the surface. Flip the dough over, gently stretch it into a rough oval, fold the sides in, then roll it towards you to form a tight boule. Place **seam-side up** in a well-floured banneton or a bowl lined with a floured tea towel.

### Cold retard

duration: 480-960

Cover tightly and refrigerate for **8–16 hours** (overnight is ideal). The cold rest develops deep flavour and makes scoring easier. The dough can safely stay in the fridge for up to 3 days.

### Preheat oven with Dutch oven

duration: 60

Place your Dutch oven (with lid) in the oven and preheat to **250°C (480°F)** fan-off. Preheat for a full hour — the Dutch oven needs to be thoroughly and evenly hot, not just the air around it.

### Score and bake (covered)

duration: 20

Take the dough **straight from the fridge** — cold dough scores more cleanly. Turn it out seam-side down onto a piece of baking paper. Score confidently with a lame or razor blade at 30–45°, one slash about 2cm deep.

Carefully lower the dough (on the paper) into the preheated Dutch oven. Put the lid on and bake for **20 minutes**.

### Bake uncovered for colour

duration: 25

Remove the lid. Reduce heat to **230°C** and bake for a further **20–25 minutes** until the crust is deep mahogany brown. Darker = more flavour. Don't pull it too early.

### Cool completely

duration: 120

Transfer to a wire rack and **wait at least 2 hours** before cutting — the crumb is still setting as the loaf cools. Cutting too early produces a gummy, dense interior no matter how good the bake was.

Tap the base: a hollow sound means it's fully baked through.
